3.1 · Skeletal System
Structure & Function
Bone
🏗️
Skeleton
Like the steel frame of a building
Provides support, protects organs, allows movement, produces blood cells (in red bone marrow), and stores minerals like calcium and phosphorus.
Bone
🔴
Red Bone Marrow
Like IMSS — produces all the "workers" (blood cells)
Found in spongy bone. Produces red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets through a process called hematopoiesis.
Bone
🟡
Yellow Bone Marrow
Like a bodega — stores fat reserves
Stores fat (adipose tissue). Found in the hollow shaft (diaphysis) of long bones. Can convert to red marrow in emergencies.
Tissue
🩹
Periosteum
Like the skin of the bone — outermost protective layer
Dense connective tissue membrane covering bone surfaces. Contains blood vessels, nerves, and osteoblasts for growth and repair.
Cell
🔨
Osteoblast
The construction workers — build bone
Cells that synthesize and deposit bone matrix. They become osteocytes once surrounded by matrix. Active during growth and fracture repair.
Cell
🏠
Osteocyte
The residents — live inside the finished bone
Mature bone cells trapped in lacunae (small cavities). Maintain the bone matrix and communicate via small channels called canaliculi.
Cell
💥
Osteoclast
The demolition crew — break down bone
Large multinucleated cells that resorb (break down) bone tissue. Crucial for remodeling and releasing calcium into the bloodstream.
Joint
🔗
Cartilage
Like the rubber pads in a car's suspension
Flexible connective tissue that cushions joints, supports structures (nose, ears), and forms the fetal skeleton before ossification. Avascular — no blood supply.
Joint
🔩
Ligament
Like a bolt connecting two metal beams — bone to bone
Dense connective tissue that connects bone to bone at joints. Stabilizes joints and prevents excessive movement.
Joint
💧
Synovial Fluid
Like the oil in an engine — reduces friction
Viscous fluid found in synovial joints. Lubricates cartilage, reduces friction, absorbs shock, and nourishes cartilage cells.
Diseases & Conditions
Osteoporosis
Metabolic disease
Loss of bone density — more osteoclast than osteoblast activity. Bones become brittle and fracture easily. Common in post-menopausal women.
Osteoarthritis
Degenerative
Cartilage between joints wears down over time. Causes pain, stiffness, and reduced range of motion. Most common in knees and hips.
Rheumatoid Arthritis
Autoimmune
Immune system attacks the synovial membrane. Causes joint inflammation, pain, and eventual bone destruction. Symmetric — affects both sides.
Fractures
Trauma
Break in bone continuity. Types: simple (clean break), compound (pierces skin), stress (repeated microtrauma), comminuted (shattered).
Scoliosis
Structural
Lateral (sideways) curvature of the spine. Can be idiopathic, congenital, or neuromuscular. Detected by uneven shoulders/hips.
Osteosarcoma
Cancer
Malignant bone tumor. Most common in teenagers during growth spurts. Typically affects long bones (femur, tibia).
3.2 · Muscular System
Structure & Function
Type
💪
Skeletal Muscle
Like a manually operated crane — voluntary control
Striated (striped), voluntary muscle attached to bones via tendons. Responsible for body movement and posture. Contains actin and myosin filaments.
Type
❤️
Cardiac Muscle
Like a diesel engine — runs 24/7 without stopping
Striated, involuntary muscle found only in the heart. Has intercalated discs for synchronized contraction. Self-stimulating (automaticity).
Type
🌀
Smooth Muscle
Like the automatic valve in a pipe — you don't control it
Non-striated, involuntary muscle in organs and vessels (stomach, intestines, blood vessels). Controls digestion, circulation, and other internal functions.
Mechanism
🪝
Sliding Filament Theory
Like rowing a boat — oars (myosin) pull the water (actin)
Myosin heads attach to actin filaments and pull them toward the center of the sarcomere. ATP provides energy. Requires Ca²⁺ ions to expose binding sites on actin.
Structure
📦
Sarcomere
The basic unit — like one brick in a wall
The functional unit of skeletal and cardiac muscle. Bounded by Z-lines. Contains thick filaments (myosin) and thin filaments (actin). Shortens during contraction.
Connection
🪢
Tendon
Like the rope connecting the crane to the load — muscle to bone
Dense connective tissue connecting muscle to bone. Transmits the force of muscular contraction to create movement.
Pairs
⬆️⬇️
Antagonistic Muscles
Like a seesaw — when one side goes up, the other goes down
Muscles that work in opposing pairs. When agonist contracts (bicep), antagonist relaxes (tricep) and vice versa. Example: biceps/triceps, hamstrings/quadriceps.
Signal
⚡
Neuromuscular Junction
Like the electric outlet — where nerve signal meets muscle
Synapse between a motor neuron and muscle fiber. Acetylcholine (ACh) is released, binds to receptors, triggers action potential → muscle contracts.
Diseases & Conditions
Muscular Dystrophy
Genetic
Group of diseases causing progressive muscle weakness. Duchenne MD (most common) affects boys — mutation in dystrophin gene. No cure, only management.
Myasthenia Gravis
Autoimmune
Antibodies block ACh receptors at neuromuscular junction. Causes muscle weakness and fatigue, worse with activity. Droopy eyelids (ptosis) is classic sign.
Tendinitis
Inflammation
Inflammation of a tendon due to overuse or injury. Common in shoulder (rotator cuff), elbow (tennis elbow), and Achilles tendon.
Muscle Cramp
Functional
Sudden, involuntary contraction of a muscle. Caused by dehydration, electrolyte imbalance (low Na⁺, K⁺, Mg²⁺), or nerve compression.
Bacterial Infections of the Muscular System
Tetanus
Clostridium tetani
Bacteria releases toxin that blocks inhibitory signals → continuous muscle contraction ("lockjaw"). Prevented by vaccine. Enters through wounds.
Necrotizing Fasciitis
Group A Streptococcus
"Flesh-eating bacteria." Rapidly destroys fascia and muscle tissue. Medical emergency requiring surgical debridement and IV antibiotics.
Botulism
Clostridium botulinum
Toxin blocks ACh release at neuromuscular junction → flaccid paralysis. Opposite of tetanus (flaccid vs. spastic). Source: improperly canned foods.
3.3 · Integumentary System
Structure & Function
Layer
🧱
Epidermis
Like the paint and plaster on a wall — outermost protective coat
Outermost layer. Avascular (no blood vessels). Made of stratified squamous epithelium. Cells produced in stratum basale, die and fill with keratin as they move up to stratum corneum.
Layer
🔌
Dermis
Like the building's infrastructure — wiring, plumbing, sensors
Middle layer. Contains blood vessels, nerves, hair follicles, sweat glands, and sebaceous glands. Made of collagen and elastin for strength and flexibility.
Layer
🛢️
Hypodermis (Subcutis)
Like the insulation under a floor — fat storage and cushioning
Deepest layer. Made mainly of adipose tissue. Insulates the body, stores energy, cushions organs, and anchors skin to underlying muscle/bone.
Pigment
🎨
Melanocytes
Like the painters — they decide the color of the skin
Cells in the stratum basale that produce melanin. Melanin absorbs UV radiation, protecting DNA. Everyone has the same number — skin color depends on melanin production rate.
Gland
💧
Sweat Glands (Eccrine)
Like the AC system — cools the body down
Produce sweat (mostly water + NaCl). Distributed across body. Evaporation cools the skin. Also helps excrete small amounts of urea and lactic acid.
Gland
🧴
Sebaceous Glands
Like the waterproofing coating on a jacket
Produce sebum (oil) that lubricates and waterproofs skin and hair. Attached to hair follicles. Overactivity → acne; blocked duct → cyst.
Function
☀️
Vitamin D Synthesis
Like a solar panel — converts sunlight into something useful
UV radiation converts 7-dehydrocholesterol in skin to Vitamin D3. Essential for calcium absorption and bone health. Liver and kidneys further activate it.
Function
🛡️
Skin as Barrier
Like a security wall — keeps pathogens, chemicals, and UV out
Physical barrier against pathogens. Slightly acidic pH (4.5–5.5) inhibits bacterial growth. Keratin prevents water loss and chemical penetration.
Diseases
Melanoma
Skin cancer
Malignant tumor of melanocytes. Most dangerous skin cancer. Check with ABCDE rule: Asymmetry, Border, Color, Diameter, Evolving.
Psoriasis
Autoimmune
Immune system speeds up skin cell turnover. Cells accumulate faster than they shed → thick, scaly plaques. Chronic, with flare-ups and remissions.
Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)
Inflammatory
Skin barrier dysfunction + immune overreaction. Causes itchy, inflamed, cracked skin. Often linked to asthma and allergies.
Acne Vulgaris
Follicular
Sebaceous gland overproduction + Cutibacterium acnes bacteria. Blocked follicles → whiteheads, blackheads, cysts. Common in adolescence.
Burns (1°/2°/3°)
Trauma
1st° — epidermis only (redness). 2nd° — epidermis + dermis (blisters). 3rd° — all layers (painless, white/black, requires skin graft).
3.4 · Circulatory System
Structure & Function
Organ
❤️
Heart
Like a water pump in a building — keeps fluid moving through the whole system
4-chambered muscular organ. Right side pumps deoxygenated blood to lungs (pulmonary circuit). Left side pumps oxygenated blood to body (systemic circuit). ~100,000 beats/day.
Vessel
🚀
Arteries
Like the main water pipes under pressure — carry blood AWAY from heart
Thick, elastic walls to withstand high pressure. Carry oxygenated blood (except pulmonary artery). No valves. The aorta is the largest artery.
Vessel
🏠
Veins
Like the drain pipes — carry blood BACK to the heart (low pressure)
Thin walls, large lumen, have valves to prevent backflow. Carry deoxygenated blood (except pulmonary vein). Venous return aided by muscle contractions and breathing.
Vessel
🕸️
Capillaries
Like the tiny drip irrigation channels — where the actual exchange happens
One cell thick (endothelium only). Exchange of O₂, CO₂, nutrients, and waste between blood and tissues. So narrow (~8 µm) that RBCs pass single-file.
Blood
🔴
Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes)
Like delivery trucks — carry oxygen using hemoglobin
Biconcave, anucleate (no nucleus in mature form). Contain hemoglobin which binds O₂. Life span ~120 days. Produced in red bone marrow.
Blood
🩹
Platelets (Thrombocytes)
Like the repair crew — seal leaks (clot blood)
Cell fragments from megakaryocytes. Aggregate at wound sites, release clotting factors, form a temporary plug. Work with fibrin to form final clot.
Cardiac
⚡
Cardiac Conduction
Like an electrical circuit — SA node is the fuse box
SA node → AV node → Bundle of His → Purkinje fibers. SA node sets heart rate (pacemaker). Signal triggers synchronized contraction of atria then ventricles.
Pressure
📊
Blood Pressure
Like water pressure in a hose — systolic (squeeze) / diastolic (relax)
Systolic: pressure during ventricular contraction. Diastolic: pressure during relaxation. Normal: 120/80 mmHg. Measured in arteries. Regulated by kidneys, hormones, heart rate.
Diseases
Hypertension
Chronic
Persistently high blood pressure (>130/80). "Silent killer" — often asymptomatic. Damages arteries, heart, kidneys, and brain over time. Major risk factor for stroke and MI.
Atherosclerosis
Vascular
Build-up of plaques (cholesterol, fat, calcium) inside arterial walls. Narrows arteries, reduces blood flow. Can cause heart attack (coronary) or stroke (cerebral).
Myocardial Infarction
Emergency
Heart attack. Coronary artery blocked (usually by clot on plaque) → heart muscle dies. Symptoms: chest pain, left arm pain, sweating, nausea. Time-critical treatment.
Anemia
Blood disorder
Low hemoglobin or RBC count → insufficient O₂ delivery. Types: iron-deficiency (most common), sickle cell (genetic), aplastic (bone marrow failure).
Leukemia
Cancer
Cancer of blood-forming cells. Overproduction of abnormal WBCs that crowd out normal cells. Types: acute/chronic, lymphocytic/myeloid.
Heart Failure
Chronic
Heart can't pump blood efficiently. Can be left-sided (fluid in lungs → dyspnea) or right-sided (fluid in legs → edema). Managed but not cured.
3.5 · Immune System
Innate vs Adaptive
Innate
🛡️
Innate Immune System
Like a guarda de seguridad — no discrimina, detiene a todos los sospechosos
First line of defense. Non-specific (attacks anything foreign). Responds within minutes-hours. Includes physical barriers (skin, mucus), inflammation, fever, phagocytes (neutrophils, macrophages), and NK cells.
Innate
🦠
Phagocytosis
Like Pac-Man — macrophages eat and destroy pathogens
Macrophages and neutrophils engulf pathogens via phagocytosis. Pathogen is enclosed in a phagosome, fused with lysosomes, and destroyed. Macrophages also present antigens to T cells.
Innate
🔥
Inflammation
Like the military setting up a perimeter — hot, swollen, redness = troops arriving
4 signs: redness (vasodilation), heat, swelling (fluid leaks out), pain. Caused by histamine, prostaglandins. Recruits immune cells to the site of infection or injury.
Adaptive
🎯
Adaptive Immune System
Like a hit squad — trained to target one specific enemy
Specific defense against particular antigens. Slower (days to weeks) but highly targeted and creates immunological memory. Involves T cells (cellular) and B cells (humoral/antibodies).
Adaptive
🅱️
B Cells & Antibodies
Like a missile factory — make specialized weapons for each enemy
B cells differentiate into plasma cells that secrete antibodies (immunoglobulins). Antibodies bind specific antigens → neutralize, agglutinate, or flag for destruction (opsonization).
T Cell
🔪
Cytotoxic T Cells (CD8+)
Like the assassins — directly kill infected cells
Kill cells infected by viruses or cancer cells. Recognize antigens on MHC class I molecules. Release perforin (punches holes in membrane) and granzymes (trigger apoptosis).
T Cell
📢
Helper T Cells (CD4+)
Like the general — coordinates the whole immune response
Recognize antigens on MHC class II (on APCs). Release cytokines to activate cytotoxic T cells and B cells. Central to adaptive immunity — HIV destroys these, causing AIDS.
Memory
🧠
Immunological Memory
Like the Most Wanted list — the immune system never forgets a face
After infection/vaccination, memory B and T cells persist for years. Second exposure → faster, stronger response (secondary immune response). Basis of vaccines.
Immunity
💉
Active Immunity
Like training for a fight yourself — your body builds its own defense
Body produces its own antibodies + memory cells. Natural: after infection. Artificial: after vaccine. Slower to develop but long-lasting (years to lifetime).
Immunity
🩸
Passive Immunity
Like borrowing someone else's bulletproof vest — works now but doesn't last
Antibodies transferred from another source. Natural: maternal antibodies via placenta/breast milk. Artificial: immunoglobulin injection (antivenom, rabies treatment). Fast but temporary — no memory formed.
Lymphatic
🌊
Lymphatic System
Like the sewer system of the body — drains excess fluid and filters it
Network of vessels that collect interstitial fluid (lymph) and return it to blood. Lymph nodes filter pathogens. Contains lymphocytes. Spleen, thymus, and tonsils are lymphoid organs.
Lymphatic
🏫
Thymus
Like a military school — where T cells get trained and selected
Gland in the chest where immature T cells (thymocytes) mature. Positive selection: T cells that recognize MHC survive. Negative selection: self-reactive T cells are destroyed. Shrinks with age.
Diseases of the Immune System
HIV/AIDS
Viral (retrovirus)
HIV destroys CD4+ Helper T cells. When count drops below 200/µL → AIDS. No immune defense → opportunistic infections (PCP, CMV). Managed with antiretrovirals (ART).
Lupus (SLE)
Autoimmune
Immune system attacks own DNA and tissues. Butterfly rash on face, joint pain, kidney damage. More common in women. "The great imitator" — mimics many diseases.
Type 1 Diabetes
Autoimmune
Immune system destroys pancreatic beta cells → no insulin produced. Requires lifelong insulin therapy. Distinct from Type 2 (insulin resistance, not autoimmune).
Anaphylaxis
Hypersensitivity
Severe allergic reaction. Massive histamine release → vasodilation, airway swelling, drop in blood pressure. Life-threatening. Treat with epinephrine (EpiPen).
SCID
Immunodeficiency
Severe Combined Immunodeficiency — absence of both T and B cells. "Bubble boy disease." Patients highly vulnerable to all infections. Treated with bone marrow transplant.
Lymphoma
Cancer
Cancer of lymphocytes. Hodgkin's (has Reed-Sternberg cells, better prognosis) vs Non-Hodgkin's (many subtypes). Presents as painless swollen lymph nodes.
